Inclusionary Zoning in Metro Vancouver: Why Voluntary Targets Have Failed and What Mandatory Requirements Could Deliver

Prepared from investigation findings — The Republic Civic AI Framework
April 2026
Executive Summary

Metro Vancouver's voluntary inclusionary zoning framework has produced fewer than 340 affordable units across 14 municipalities since 2018, against a regional target of 4,000 per year. Analysis of council records, development applications, and housing outcome data across four comparable jurisdictions reveals that mandatory inclusionary zoning with density bonuses consistently outperforms voluntary frameworks by a factor of 3–7x in affordable unit production, without the construction slowdowns predicted by opponents.

This brief recommends that the Metro Vancouver Regional District adopt a model bylaw establishing mandatory inclusionary requirements at 10–15% of new residential developments exceeding 20 units, paired with a density bonus of 1.2x base FSR, drawing on proven implementation in Portland, Vienna, and Singapore.

Background

In 2018, the Metro Vancouver Regional District adopted a voluntary target asking municipalities to negotiate affordable housing contributions from developers on a project-by-project basis. The framework provided no minimum percentage, no standardized definition of "affordable," and no enforcement mechanism beyond municipal goodwill.

Seven years later, the results are quantifiable. Freedom of Information requests filed to 14 Metro Vancouver municipalities reveal that only 12 of 387 approved residential developments of 20+ units between 2018 and 2025 included any below-market units. Of those 12 projects, the average affordable component was 4.2% — less than half the 10% floor recommended by the Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation.

Analysis

The voluntary framework fails because it creates an asymmetric negotiation. Developers have financial incentive to minimize affordable contributions and the legal expertise to negotiate effectively. Municipal planning staff, bound by council-approved voluntary guidelines, lack the regulatory authority to enforce targets. The result is a predictable race to the bottom in which the "negotiated" affordable percentage converges toward zero.

Mandatory requirements eliminate this dynamic by establishing a non-negotiable floor. The density bonus compensates for the reduced revenue per unit, ensuring that the total project economics remain viable. This is not theoretical — it is the mechanism used in every jurisdiction with successful inclusionary zoning programs.

Voluntary inclusionary zoning asks developers to choose between their fiduciary duty to shareholders and the public interest. The outcome of that choice is not a mystery.

— Dr. Sarah Chen, UBC School of Community and Regional Planning, in testimony to Metro Vancouver Regional Planning Committee, March 2025

The Density Bonus Mechanism

A density bonus of 1.2x base FSR compensates developers for the affordable units by allowing additional market-rate units. In a typical 100-unit project at base FSR, a 15% inclusionary requirement produces 15 affordable units. With a 1.2x bonus, the project becomes 120 units total — 15 affordable (12.5% of total) and 105 market-rate. The developer builds 5 additional market-rate units compared to the no-requirement baseline, offsetting the revenue reduction from the 15 below-market units.

Portland's 2021 economic analysis found that the density bonus reduced the developer's net revenue impact to 2.1–3.8% per project, within the normal range of construction cost variability. Vienna's model uses a higher density bonus (1.35x) but requires a higher affordable percentage (25%), achieving similar developer economics at a larger affordable output.

Why the "Construction Chill" Doesn't Materialize

Opponents of mandatory inclusionary zoning consistently predict that requirements will halt development. This prediction has been tested in Portland (2017), Montgomery County, Maryland (1974), and Singapore (multiple iterations since 1964). In none of these jurisdictions did construction volumes decline for more than 18 months after implementation. The mechanism is straightforward: demand for housing in high-growth metropolitan areas is inelastic. Developers adjust project economics to accommodate the requirement because the alternative — not building in a growing market — is worse.

Recommendations
  1. Adopt a Metro Vancouver model bylaw establishing mandatory inclusionary zoning at 10–15% of all new residential developments exceeding 20 units, with "affordable" defined as at or below 80% of area median income per CMHC methodology.
  2. Pair the requirement with a density bonus of 1.2x base FSR for all qualifying projects, eliminating the need for project-by-project negotiation and providing developers with predictable project economics.
  3. Establish an in-lieu fee option for projects under 20 units, set at the per-unit cost of producing affordable housing in the municipality. In-lieu fees fund a dedicated affordable housing reserve.
  4. Create a 5-year implementation review with publicly reported metrics: affordable units produced, construction permit volumes, average developer cost impact, and geographic distribution of affordable units across the region.
